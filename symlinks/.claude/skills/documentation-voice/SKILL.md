# Documentation voice

Write so a busy reader gets the point fast and trusts it. These rules shape how the prose
*sounds*. Plain, direct, human. Succinct is not curt, so cut words but keep the warmth.

This skill is about voice, not structure. It says nothing about what sections a doc needs. It
only governs how the sentences read.

## Keep this skill alive

This is a living skill. When we sharpen a rule or settle a new one while writing, update this file
in the same pass. A lesson that lives only in a chat is a lesson we'll relearn.

## Principles

1. **Lead with the point.** State the rule or answer in the first sentence, then give the reason.
   Make headings full statements, not labels. Write "Color has purpose," not "Color usage." Keep a
   heading to one scannable line with no trailing punctuation.
2. **One idea per sentence, and keep them short.** Prefer subject, verb, object. If a sentence runs
   past about 25 words or has two "and"s, split it.
3. **Active voice, present tense.** The thing does the thing. Write "Color signals status," not
   "Status is signaled by color."
4. **Talk to the reader, and use imperatives for guidance.** Write "Lead with a verb." Write "Cut
   the filler." Call the reader "you" and the team "we." Never put words in the reader's mouth with
   "I" or "my."
5. **Weigh every word.** Cut anything that doesn't change the meaning. That means filler, hedges,
   throat-clearing, and most adverbs. Every extra word is noise.
6. **Be concrete.** Ground every claim in an example, a value, or a case. Show the word, the number,
   the do and the don't. Abstraction is where docs go to die.
7. **Give the why in the same breath.** Pair a rule with its reason in one line. "Use red only for
   destructive actions, so it keeps its weight."
8. **Sound human, not corporate or clever.** Use contractions like "you're," "don't," and "it's."
   They read like a person. Prefer plain words over jargon. Cut marketing adjectives and exclamation
   points. If you wouldn't say it to a colleague, rewrite it.
9. **Punctuate like speech.** Don't join clauses with em dashes, en dashes, colons, or semicolons.
   They sound formal, not human. Break the thought into short sentences. Commas and parentheses do
   the rest.
10. **Cut words that need explaining.** If a word makes the reader pause or feels stuffy, swap it for
    the plain one. "Apparatus" becomes "the extra chrome." "Forgo" becomes "skip." A word you'd have
    to elaborate is the wrong word.
11. **Keep series parallel.** Items in a list or a sentence share grammatical form. They read with
    rhythm and scan fast.
12. **Default to confidence.** Make claims as statements, not suggestions. Hedge with "usually" or
    "often" only when you're genuinely unsure.

## The page tagline, the one allowed flourish

At the very top of a page or major section, you can open with a single evocative sentence that
frames the topic by what it *does*. This is the one place the lean rules relax, so a tagline can be
a little richer and rhythmic. Everything below it returns to plain, rule-first prose.

- **Shape.** Present tense, active, usually a parallel triple. "[Subject] [does X], [does Y], and
  [does Z]." It describes the topic's purpose, not its mechanics.
- **Examples (Polaris).** "Color highlights important areas, communicates status and urgency, and
  directs attention." Or "Depth introduces realism, establishes visual hierarchy, and creates focus."
- **One sentence. No more.** If it needs two, it's body prose, not a tagline.
- **Only where a high-level frame helps.** Conceptual or foundational pages earn one. Reference,
  utility, and how-to pages don't, since the reader just wants the thing.
- **Don't let the style leak past the opener.** The body stays lean and direct.

## Revise pass, run on every paragraph

- Does the first sentence work alone as the takeaway?
- Any sentence over about 25 words, or with two "and"s? Split it.
- Any passive voice? Flip it so the actor leads.
- Any em dash, en dash, colon, or semicolon joining clauses? Make them separate sentences.
- Any word you can delete without losing meaning? Delete it.
- Any word that needs explaining or sounds stuffy? Swap it for the plain one.
- Any abstract claim with no concrete example? Add one or cut the claim.
- Read it aloud. Does it sound like a person talking, not a press release?

## Cut these on sight

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| in order to | to |
| utilize, leverage | use |
| facilitate, enable, allow you to | lets you, or a direct verb |
| forgo | skip |
| apparatus | the extra chrome, the extra parts |
| there is, there are X that… | name X and give it a verb |
| it's important to note that | (delete, just say it) |
| in general, for the most part, typically | (delete unless load-bearing) |
| very, really, quite, actually, simply, just | (usually delete) |
| make a decision, provide support for | decide, support |
| clause — aside — clause | two sentences, or commas |
| clause: clause, clause; clause | two sentences |

## Before, after

- "The utilization of color should be done in a purposeful manner." → **"Use color on purpose."**
- "It is recommended that buttons be labeled clearly so that users are able to understand them."
  → **"Label buttons clearly so people understand them."**
- "There are a number of different states that an interaction can have." → **"An interaction has
  several states."**
- "A page brings apparatus — breadcrumbs, navigation, more detail — that a modal lets you forgo."
  → **"A modal lets you skip page-level chrome like breadcrumbs and navigation. A page gives you that
  chrome when the content needs it."**

## One caution

Don't over-trim into a robot. The goal is plain and human, not terse and cold. Keep the connective
words that make prose flow, like "but," "so," and "which means," when they earn their place. Just
don't pad. When in doubt, read it aloud. If it sounds like a person being helpful, it's right.
